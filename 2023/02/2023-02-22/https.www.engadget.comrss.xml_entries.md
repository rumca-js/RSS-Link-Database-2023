## FCC chair proposes rules to reduce scam robotexts

<p>The chair of the <a href="https://www.engadget.com/tag/fcc/"><ins>Federal Trade Commission</ins></a> has proposed new rules to tackle the scourge of text message scams. If the agency's commissioners approve the rules at a meeting in March, providers would have to block <a href="https://www.engadget.com/2018-12-12-fcc-ruling-allows-blocking-spam-texts.html"><ins>robotexts</ins></a> that are &quot;highly likely to be illegal,&quot; chair Jessica Rosenworcel said in a statement.</p><p>The FCC has yet to publish the full text of Rosenworcel's proposal. If adopted, it will force providers to block text messages that appear to be from numbers on a do-not-originate list. They include unused, invalid and unallocated numbers, as well as those that government agencies and &quot;other well-known entities&quot; say they don't send texts from. Messages from these numbers &quot;are highly likely to be illegal and no consumer would want to receive them,&quot; an FCC statement reads.</p><span id="end-legacy-contents"></span><p>Moreover, providers would have to block texts from entities that the FCC flags for sending illegal robotexts. The proposed rules state that providers would additionally need to expand <a href="https://www.donotcall.gov/"><ins>National Do Not Call Registry</ins></a> protections to include texts to prevent consumers from receiving unwanted marketing messages.</p><p>‚ÄúMissing packages that don‚Äôt exist; confirmation of payments that didn‚Äôt happen; links to shady websites; and truncated ‚Äòwrong number‚Äô messages from strangers. These scam robotexts are a part of everyday life for too many of us,‚Äù Rosenworcel said. ‚ÄúI‚Äôm asking my colleagues to join me in adopting the first FCC rules to focus on shutting down scam texts. But we‚Äôre not stopping here. Because we are going to keep at it and develop more ways to take on this growing consumer threat.‚Äù</p><p>The robotext proposal follows measures the FCC has taken to <a href="https://www.engadget.com/robocall-company-may-receive-the-largest-fine-ever-from-the-fcc-110759522.html"><ins>stamp out robocalls</ins></a>. Both issues are <a href="https://www.fcc.gov/news-events/notes/2023/02/22/march-2023-open-meeting-agenda">on the agenda</a> for the FCC's open meeting next month, along with other items like a proposed framework &quot;for increased collaboration between terrestrial mobile network operators and satellite service providers&quot; to bolster phone service in areas where it is lacking. That could could come in useful for life-or-death situations in remote areas. Certain devices, such as <a href="https://www.engadget.com/iphone-14-emergency-sos-satellite-connection-183230405.html">the iPhone 14</a>, now offer satellite connectivity for emergency use.</p>

## Apple is reportedly closer to bringing no-prick glucose monitoring to the Watch
 - [https://www.engadget.com/apple-watch-no-prick-blood-glucose-monitor-200137031.html?src=rss](https://www.engadget.com/apple-watch-no-prick-blood-glucose-monitor-200137031.html?src=rss)

<p>Apple's long-running quest to bring <a href="https://www.engadget.com/apple-watch-blood-sugar-rockley-photonics-212241562.html">blood glucose monitoring</a> to the Apple Watch appears to be moving forward. <em>Bloomberg</em><a href="https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-02-22/apple-watch-blood-glucose-monitor-could-revolutionize-diabetes-care-aapl?sref=10lNAhZ9">sources</a> claim the company's no-prick monitoring is now at a &quot;proof-of-concept stage&quot; and good enough that it could come to market once it's smaller. The technology, which uses lasers to gauge glucose concentration under the skin, was previously tabletop sized but has reportedly advanced to the point where an iPhone-sized wearable prototype is in the works.</p><p>The system would not only help people with diabetes monitor their conditions, but would ideally alert people who are prediabetic, the insiders say. They could then make changes that prevent Type 2 (adult onset) diabetes.</p><span id="end-legacy-contents"></span><p>Apple declined to comment. The project has supposedly been in development for a long time. It began in 2010, when an ailing Steve Jobs had his company buy blood glucose monitoring startup RareLight. Apple is said to have kept the effort secret by operating it as a seemingly isolated firm, Avolonte Health, but folded it into a previously unknown Exploratory Design Group (XDG). CEO Tim Cook, Apple Watch hardware lead Eugene Kim and other top leaders have been involved.</p><p>Any real-world product is likely years away, according to <em>Bloomberg</em>. The industry also doesn't have a great track record of bringing no-prick monitors to market. In 2018, Alphabet's health subsidiary Verily <a href="https://www.engadget.com/2018-11-16-verily-shelves-glucose-monitoring-contact-lens-project.html">scrapped plans for a smart contact lens</a> that would have tracked glucose using tears. Even major brands with vast resources aren't guaranteed success, in other words, and it's not clear how accurate Apple's solution would be.</p><p>There are strong incentives to bring this tech to wearables. The Apple Watch is frequently marketed as a health device and can spot signs of atrial fibrillation, low blood oxygen levels and (as of <a href="https://www.engadget.com/apple-watch-series-8-real-world-test-130031046.html">Series 8</a>) ovulation cycles. Non-intrusive glucose monitoring could make it an indispensable tool for those with diabetes ‚Äî you wouldn't need a <a href="https://www.engadget.com/2015-08-27-the-dexcom-g5-lets-diabetics-track-blood-sugar-on-their-phone.html">dedicated device</a> that invades your skin, such as a continuous glucose sensor that sends info from an electrode-equipped thin needle to an external receiver. That painless approach could give the Apple Watch an edge over competing smartwatches.</p>
