## Why do South Koreans despise China? Let them speak for themselves...

 - date published: 2023-01-27 15:18:30+00:00
 - user: None

<img src="https://www.louderwithcrowder.com/media-library/image.jpg?id=32913430&amp;width=1245&amp;height=700&amp;coordinates=0%2C0%2C0%2C0" /><br /><br /><p><em> ‚ÄúSouth Koreans hold the world‚Äôs most negative views on China.‚Äù</em></p><p>In December 2022, research from the Sinophone Borderlands Project revealed South Koreans hold overwhelmingly negative views of their giant neighbor. Overall, 81% of South Korean respondents stated that they had very negative or somewhat negative views of China, by far the largest percentage out of the 56 countries surveyed. <a href="https://ceias.eu/wp-content/uploads/2022/12/Europe_and_North_America_Draft-FINAL.pdf" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">The next closes country was Switzerland at 72%, nearly ten points behind.</a></p><p>Somewhat more surprising is that South Koreans‚Äô views of China are even more negative than those they hold regarding more traditionally antagonistic countries <a href="https://ceias.eu/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/Korean-poll-finaldraft-adjustments-3-1_rqt-1-2.pdf" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">like North Korea (69%) and Japan (62%).</a></p><p>These negative perceptions of China shouldn‚Äôt necessarily come as a surprise given Beijing‚Äôs inflammatory behavior across several domains.</p><p>Domestically, China has instituted mass surveillance and internment against Uyghur Muslims in Xinjiang, quashed democratic movements in Hong Kong, and instituted some of the world‚Äôs most draconian measures in response to the COVID-19 pandemic. In the foreign policy realm, Beijing has rapidly accelerated its military buildup while constantly threating aggressive force against Taiwan, laid false claim to copious amounts of territory in the South China Sea, <a href="https://www.wsj.com/articles/china-belt-road-debt-11663961638" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">saddled developing countries with unsustainable debt traps</a>, and can certainly be blamed for the initial outbreak and handling of the aforementioned COVID pandemic.</p><p>And of course, each of these policy decisions has been buttressed by hard-headed bully rhetoric that China refers to as ‚Äú<a href="https://thediplomat.com/2020/05/interpreting-chinas-wolf-warrior-diplomacy/" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Wolf Warrior</a>‚Äù diplomacy, which until recently dominated the Ministry of Foreign Affairs.</p><p>South Korea is not by any means unique in its disdain for China. A 2020 <a href="https://www.pewresearch.org/global/2020/10/06/unfavorable-views-of-china-reach-historic-highs-in-many-countries/" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Pew report</a> titled ‚ÄúUnfavorable Views of China Reach Historic Highs in Many Countries‚Äù showed that public opinion on China was taking a nose dive nearly everywhere across the developed world. In fact, the median response across the 14 countries surveyed was 73% unfavorable to only 24% favorable.</p><p>What <em>does</em> make South Koreans unique is their particularly harsh view of the Chinese people, specifically. According to a recent <a href="https://thediplomat.com/2022/12/south-koreans-have-the-worlds-most-negative-views-of-china-why/" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">article</a> from The Diplomat, Europeans and Americans tend to hold separate views of the Chinese Communist Party (CCP) and the Chinese people, having generally positive views of the latter.</p><p>However, South Koreans seem to show little differentiation between the Chinese People and the CCP, with 77% of respondents having negative or very negative views of the Chinese populace.</p><p>So, what explains South Korea‚Äôs particularly high level of disdain for China? To find out I spoke with four Koreans and asked them the following questions.</p><ul>
<li>1.What are the biggest factors that have resulted in South Koreans having the world‚Äôs most negative views of China?</li>
<li>2.Why do Koreans not only view the CCP negatively, but also the Chinese people?</li>
</ul><p>
	The Koreans I interviewed are all college educated and range in age from 20-32, thus their views may not be representative of the entire population. Nonetheless, their answers provide much needed context and narrative to supplement the figures displayed in recent polling. Pseudonyms are used for anonymity purposes, but personal details such as occupation and age are accurately disclosed.
</p><p>
	ENVIRONMENT
</p><p>
	The most prominent answers given for Koreans‚Äô negative views of China were related to pollution and the environment, specifically Yellow Dust.
</p><p>
	Won-bin Kim, a 27-year-old graduate student, noted this as the most pressing issue surrounding South Korea ‚Äì China relations. ‚ÄúI can‚Äôt generalize the answer, but for me the biggest issue is the environment,‚Äù said Mr. Kim. ‚ÄúWhen I was young, Yellow Dust should [sic] come like three to four days at Spring, but now in Korea, it‚Äôs hard to find a day without Yellow Dust in the spring.‚Äù
</p><p>
	Yellow dust, or <em>hwangsa </em>in Korean, is a phenomenon where fine dust from the deserts of Northern China and Mongolia is blown by strong winds onto the Korean Peninsula. When the fine dust enters the lungs, it can cause sore throat and other respiratory issues such as trouble breathing. This dust is also capable of bringing with it industrial pollutants such as pesticides.
</p><p>
	In truth the amount of Yellow Dust in South Korea has fluctuated year to year, with a steady drop in duration occurring between <a href="https://www.statista.com/statistics/1125927/south-korea-yellow-dust-duration/" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">2015 and 2020</a>. However, 2021 did mark a six-year high with 22 days of Yellow Dust cover.
</p><p>
