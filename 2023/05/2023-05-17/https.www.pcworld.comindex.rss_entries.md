## Intel reveals which CPU/GPU combo offers the best bang for your buck
 - [https://www.pcworld.com/article/1921040/intel-reveals-which-cpu-gpu-combo-offers-the-best-bang-for-your-buck.html](https://www.pcworld.com/article/1921040/intel-reveals-which-cpu-gpu-combo-offers-the-best-bang-for-your-buck.html)

<p>Intel&rsquo;s new Arc Balanced Builds bundles address perhaps the most fundamental question in PC building: How can you get the most performance from a PC without overpaying?</p>



<p>Intel knows, at least where its own processors and Arc GPUs are concerned. And, based upon the results of its own (massive) internal tests, the company is working with retailers and system builders to discount bundles of its CPUs and GPUs to match the best combination for your buck.</p>



<p>PCs are rarely in &ldquo;balance&rdquo;: Some components are simply faster than others, so the flow of data from an SSD or hard drive through a motherboard&rsquo;s chipset to a CPU, back and forth to memory, and out to the GPU is inevitably limited by a component. Upgrading that component simply passes the bottleneck to elsewhere else in the system. What enthusiast and consumer sites like PCWorld try to do when testing a CPU is use the most powerful GPU possible, in the hope that the CPU will be the bottleneck. When testing a GPU, the same logic applies: We use the fastest CPU we can get.</p>



<p>In the real world, that&rsquo;s a bad strategy. There&rsquo;s no reason to pair a budget GPU with the fastest Core i9, because the GPU simply can&rsquo;t keep up. Ideally, then, you try to build a PC where the two components can scale up, hand in hand. </p>



<p>What Intel did internally was compare its Core microprocessors with its own Arc GPUs, testing them repeatedly across various configurations of CPUs and GPUs, as well as with various games, to try and answer the question of which CPU-GPU combination offered the most value without sacrificing performance <em>or </em>money. </p>



<p>The answer? The Arc A750/A770 pairs best with a Core i5 and Core i7, while the A380 really works best with a Core i3, and possibly a Core i5. While you&rsquo;re free to pair an A380 (or any Arc GPU) with any Intel Core CPU, exceeding Intel&rsquo;s recommendations will net you diminishing returns unless you upgrade the GPU.</p>

## Overwatch 2 abandons PvE Hero mode, completing the bait-and-switch
 - [https://www.pcworld.com/article/1920679/overwatch-2-abandons-pve-mode-completing-the-bait-and-switch.html](https://www.pcworld.com/article/1920679/overwatch-2-abandons-pve-mode-completing-the-bait-and-switch.html)
 - RSS feed: https://www.pcworld.com/index.rss
 - date published: 2023-05-17 17:29:41+00:00

<div id="link_wrapped_content">
<section class="wp-block-bigbite-multi-title"><div class="container"></div></section>



<p>In 2019 Blizzard announced <em>Overwatch 2</em>, an overhauled version of the original game bursting at the seams with the story-rich player-versus-environment content fans had been asking for since the original game&rsquo;s launch. In 2022 <em>Overwatch 2</em> finally came out&hellip;as a free-to-play title, lacking the cooperative mode so heavily featured at the Blizzcon debut, promising players would see it added later while the team battle mode launched. Now Blizzard has admitted it&rsquo;s not going to release a PVE mode for<em> Overwatch 2</em> at all.</p>



<p>In <a href="https://go.redirectingat.com/?id=111346X1569483&amp;url=https://www.gamespot.com/articles/overwatch-2s-pve-mode-is-being-scrapped-blizzard-explains-what-happened-and-why/1100-6514242/&amp;xcust=2-1-1920679-1-0-0&amp;sref=https://www.pcworld.com/feed" rel="nofollow">an interview with Gamespot</a>, <em>Overwatch 2</em> director Aaron Keller said that the game would feature &ldquo;part of what the team has set out to do, but not the entirety of what was discussed back at BlizzCon 2019.&rdquo; Future &ldquo;seasons&rdquo; of <em>Overwatch 2</em> content will include some PvE story missions, as the original game did, but the dedicated co-op Hero mode with dynamic character and ability progression is off the table. <em>Overwatch 2</em>, like <em>Overwatch </em>before it, will be almost entirely focused on its team-versus-team shooter mode. </p>



<p>And with the co-op mode&rsquo;s death, perhaps the biggest change between <em>Overwatch </em>and <em>Overwatch 2</em> is its unasked-for new monetization system&mdash;which is a lot less player-friendly now than it used to be.</p>



<h2 id="waiting-for-stories-that-will-never-be-told">Waiting for stories that will never be told</h2>



<p>A vocal portion of <em>Overwatch </em>players past and present aren&rsquo;t happy with the news. <em>Overwatch 2</em> is both a sequel and a replacement for the original game &mdash; the original <em>Overwatch </em>is no longer playable, despite selling over 50 million copies. <em>Overwatch 2</em> was &ldquo;sold&rdquo; (if you can use the term for a free-to-play game) to its audience as an upgrade, largely predicated on this new PvE mode that would bring fresh content and storytelling to a world that had become stagnant after adding so many fresh, vibrant elements. Now we&rsquo;ll never see that story content, or at least, we&rsquo;ll only see a very small portion of what was promised. </p>


<div class="extendedBlock-wrapper block-coreImage undefined"><figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img alt="Reinhardt PvE skill tree" class="wp-image-1921025" height="675" src="https://b2c-contenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/reinhardt-skill-tree-overwatch-2.jpg?quality=50&amp;strip=all&amp;w=1200" width="1200" /></figure><p class="imageCredit">Activision-Blizzard</p></div>



<p>This exacerbates <a href="https://www.pcworld.com/article/1359589/why-i-quit-overwatch-2-breakup-letter.html">the primary issues that I had with<em> Overwatch 2</em> back at launch</a>: That it&rsquo;s using some absolutely amazing character and production design, animation, and voice work, and never letting those characters tell a story beyond a bit of thin lore. <em>Overwatch </em>characters <em>can</em>&lsquo;<em>t </em>tell stories in the main team-based mode, because they&rsquo;re infinitely variable and replayable. They basically treat the characters as a kid&rsquo;s action figures, smashing them together for the gameplay without any further goals. </p>



<p>That&rsquo;s fine for a game that&rsquo;s only a team shooter (or, indeed, a vehicle for a multi-billion-dollar esports league). But for a game that keeps teasing us with character-focused animated shorts, it&rsquo;s infuriating. The first trailer for the original <em>Overwatch </em>set up a very <em>GI Joe</em>-esque conflict between the Overwatch &ldquo;good guys&rdquo; and Talon &ldquo;bad guys,&rdquo; and the <em>Overwatch 2</em> announcement trailer teased epic cooperative fights against an implacable computer-controlled foe. Both of those trailers now feel like dishonest representations of the games we actually received. </p>



<figure class="wp-block-embed is-type-video is-provider-youtube wp-block-embed-youtube wp-embed-aspect-16-9 wp-has-aspect-ratio"><div class="wp-block-embed__wrapper">

</div></figure>



<p>See all those characters with histories and relationships, working together to protect neo-Paris from a giant mecha? Good, because you&rsquo;ll never see it in<em> Overwatch 2</em> gameplay.</p>



<p>Keller says that at least some of that story content is still being planned. In Season 6 of <em>Overwatch 2</em>, single-player &ldquo;Hero Mastery&rdquo; mini-modes and three different event modes will be part of &ldquo;this big narrative where players do get to find out more about <em>Overwatch</em>, and they do get to find out more about what&rsquo;s happening in the world and what&rsquo;s happening with a lot of the characters in the game.&rdquo; Keller also describes new cinematics and a lore codex&hellip;but cinematics and lore weren&rsquo;t something the game was lacking. A dedicated medium for telling a story, one that doesn&rsquo;t reset after ten minutes of shooting, was. </p>



<h2 id="the-free-to-play-bait-and-switch">The free-to-play bait-and-switch</h2>



<p>And that&rsquo;s just the disappointing part of the gameplay and story. The other big change to<em> Overwatch 2 </em>was a shift to a free-to-play model, one that players didn&rsquo;t ask for and didn&rsquo;t receive gracefully. Arguably the original <em>Overwatch </em>felt somewhat like a free-to-play game (with a $40 price tag) thanks to its stingy randomized loot box system. But the remixed F2P model of <em>Overwatch 2</em> locked the full roster of interchangeable heroes, the signature feature of the original <em>Overwatch</em>, behind purchases and battle passes. It&rsquo;s more or less the same as <em>League of Legends</em>, or any number of free-to-play multiplayer mobile games you&rsquo;d care to mention. </p>



<p>In hindsight, it seems like the launch of <em>Overwatch 2</em> and the end of the original game was a decision made almost entirely to force a free-to-play model on existing players, while bringing in new players to inject cash to the never-ending live service model. As a mega-fan of the original game, who&rsquo;s been desperate for new and substantive story content for years, it&rsquo;s hard to see this as anything but a betrayal. </p>


<div class="extendedBlock-wrapper block-coreImage undefined"><figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img alt="Overwatch 2 store page" class="wp-image-1921041" height="679" src="https://b2c-contenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/overwatch-2-in-game-shop.jpg?quality=50&amp;strip=all&amp;w=1200" width="1200" /><figcaption><p>Oh, sorry, did you want some character development for this cast we&rsquo;ve been building for the better part of a decade? Best I can do is two skins for $22.&nbsp;</p>
</figcaption></figure><p class="imageCredit">Activision-Blizzard</p></div>



<p>The executives in charge of <em>Overwatch </em>can say all they want about focusing resources and player experience. It&rsquo;s clear that <em>Overwatch </em>now exists to service its in-game store, not to deliver an engaging experience to players. Perhaps it was naive to expect anything else once the free-to-play structure of the game was revealed&hellip;but that wasn&rsquo;t made clear until years after the PvE mode was so heavily featured at Blizzcon, even going so far as to be a playable demo for attending fans and media. </p>



<p>With <em>Warcraft, Starcraft, </em>and <em>Diablo</em>, Blizzard built a legacy on decades of games with incredible quality and some of the best stories ever told in the medium. <em>Overwatch</em> was sold on that reputation, a new property and universe from a developer that gives them out so sparingly. But the story side of the equation never balanced out, and isn&rsquo;t interested in trying anymore. Between this broken promise, and a marked decrease in both quality and value with recent releases, Blizzard&rsquo;s legacy has never seemed more in peril&mdash;an ominous sign with <em>Diablo IV</em> set to release in mere weeks.</p>

<p>According <a href="https://go.redirectingat.com/?id=111346X1569483&amp;url=https://blog.google/technology/safety-security/updating-our-inactive-account-policies/&amp;xcust=2-3-1919355-1-0-0&amp;sref=https://www.pcworld.com/feed" rel="nofollow">to a blog post</a>, Google reserves the right to delete the entirety of the fallow account, including your Gmail, Drive, YouTube, and Photos content. <p>To avoid losing an account to inactivity, most people will need to either sign into the account or use one of Google&rsquo;s services. <h2 id="what-is-the-screen-on-the-asus-azoth-for">What is the screen on the Asus Azoth for? </h2>



<p>Let&rsquo;s focus on that screen, since it&rsquo;s the most obvious standout feature. It&rsquo;s about two inches diagonal, black and white only&mdash;because color would just be ridiculous, right?&mdash;and paired with what Asus calls a &ldquo;control knob&rdquo; sitting on the right side of the board. This includes a little toggle that can also click in, in addition to a big, round side button. It is NOT a wheel, though it&rsquo;s where you&rsquo;d expect a wheel to be on a keyboard like this.&nbsp;</p>


<div class="extendedBlock-wrapper block-coreImage undefined"><figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img alt="Asus ROG Azoth OLED screen" class="wp-image-1915760" height="676" src="https://b2c-contenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/DSC01855.jpg?quality=50&amp;strip=all&amp;w=1200" width="1200" /></figure><p class="imageCredit">Michael Crider/Foundry</p></div>



<p>The screen switches between pre-programmed functions with a press of the big side button, at which point the toggle does different things. Pressing the button will cycle through volume adjustment, multimedia controls, lighting brightness, OLED screen brightness, and the keyboard&rsquo;s RGB lighting effect.&nbsp;</p>



<p>You might be thinking, &ldquo;Hey, there&rsquo;s nothing in there that I can&rsquo;t control with standard function keys.&rdquo; In which case, you&rsquo;re right! But on top of a new and more visual way to control all those functions, when not in active use the OLED screen can display system info like your CPU and GPU temperatures, show a music equalizer, a bit of custom scrolling text, or in its most superfluous mode, a pre-made or custom animation. These are, basically, screen savers.&nbsp;</p>



<p>Frankly, I found the screen an extremely superfluous addition to the keyboard. The only real benefit is a constant readout of system information, which can just as easily be shown in-game overlays on the monitor&mdash;you know, that thing your eyes are <em>already looking at</em>.&nbsp;</p>


<div class="extendedBlock-wrapper block-coreImage undefined"><figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img alt="Asus ROG Azoth control knob" class="wp-image-1915767" height="676" src="https://b2c-contenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/05/DSC01887b.jpg?quality=50&amp;strip=all&amp;w=1200" width="1200" /></figure><p class="imageCredit">Michael Crider/Foundry</p></div>

<h2 id="asus-azoth-switch-lubing-kit">Asus Azoth switch lubing kit</h2>

But seen as an inclusion for a set that&rsquo;s meant as an entry point for ultimate customization, it&rsquo;s a nice bonus. Perhaps not so nice that I would have paid extra for it, but I really doubt that&rsquo;s the case here&mdash;if anything&rsquo;s pushing the price up it&rsquo;s that screen.&nbsp;</p>

## How many websites are on the Internet?
 - [https://www.pcworld.com/article/1804213/how-many-websites-are-on-the-internet.html](https://www.pcworld.com/article/1804213/how-many-websites-are-on-the-internet.html)
 - RSS feed: https://www.pcworld.com/index.rss
 - date published: 2023-05-17 10:45:00+00:00

<div id="link_wrapped_content">
<section class="wp-block-bigbite-multi-title"><div class="container"></div></section>



<p>The Internet is an ever-expanding virtual universe. With each passing second, millions of users across the globe interact, share, and create content on an astonishing number of websites. But have you ever stopped to wonder just how many websites exist?</p>



<p>The internet has come a long way since the days of <a href="https://shop-links.co/link/?url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.techtarget.com%2Fsearchnetworking%2Fdefinition%2FARPANET&amp;publisher_slug=pcworld&amp;exclusive=1&amp;article_name=pcworld&amp;article_url=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.pcworld.com%2Ffeed&amp;u1=2-1-1804213-1-0-0" rel="nofollow">ARPANET</a> and the <a href="https://go.redirectingat.com/?id=111346X1569483&amp;url=http://info.cern.ch/&amp;xcust=2-1-1804213-1-0-0&amp;sref=https://www.pcworld.com/feed" rel="nofollow">first website ever created</a>. Today, we rely on it for everything from communication and entertainment to education and commerce. This insatiable appetite for connectivity has led to an explosion in the number of websites worldwide.</p>



<h2 id="what-is-a-website">What is a website?</h2>



<p><a href="https://www.pcworld.com/article/1448961/build-your-first-website-without-coding-in-just-15-minutes.html">A website</a> is a collection of related web pages, multimedia content, and other digital assets hosted on a web server and can be accessed through the Internet. It&rsquo;s like a virtual book, where each web page is a separate page within that book.</p>



<p>Websites have unique addresses called URLs, which help users find and visit them. These websites are stored on computers called web servers, which deliver the content to a web browser (like Chrome or Firefox) when someone requests it. Websites often serve a specific purpose, such as providing information, entertainment, or a platform for communication, and can be created using various web technologies.</p>



<h2 id="active-vs-inactive-websites">Active vs. inactive websites</h2>



<p>The distinction between active and inactive websites provides a clearer understanding of the actual size and scope of the online landscape. So when you consider how large the Internet actually is, also consider how many of those websites are just standing idly by.</p>



<p>Active websites are those that are regularly updated and maintained by their owners or administrators. These can be blogs, news sites, online stores, social media platforms, educational resources, and more. Active websites often see frequent user engagement, like visits, clicks, comments, and shares. They also tend to rank higher in search engine results due to their updated content, better SEO, and regular user activity.</p>



<p>Inactive websites are those that haven&rsquo;t been updated or maintained for an extended period. These websites may have outdated or irrelevant content, broken links, or limited user interaction. In some cases, the website owner may have abandoned the site, or the domain registration may have expired, meaning that you can&rsquo;t even access the website. Inactive websites can also include parked domains, which is like buying a piece of property and not doing anything with it.</p>



<p>When you think of the total number of websites on the Internet, you should consider the number of active websites as a more meaningful metric. Active websites provide a better representation of the current state of the Internet and its dynamic landscape. Inactive websites contribute to the overall count but aren&rsquo;t usually relevant or useful to users.</p>



<h2 id="the-challenges-in-counting-websites-accurately">The challenges in counting websites accurately</h2>



<p>Counting the number of websites accurately presents several challenges due to how rapidly changing the Internet is by nature. One of the many difficulties lies in defining what a website is to begin with. Are subdomains considered websites? What about single-page sites? What&rsquo;s the criteria?</p>



<p>Another challenge is the constant flux of websites being created and deleted. New websites are launched every day, while others become inactive, are taken down, or have their domain registrations expire. This constant churn makes it difficult to keep an up-to-date count of websites at any given moment.</p>



<p>To make things even more complicated, there&rsquo;s also the presence of the deep web and the dark web to consider. The deep web consists of content not indexed by standard search engines, such as private databases and intranets. The dark web, <a href="https://www.pcworld.com/article/447091/meet-darknet-the-hidden-anonymous-underbelly-of-the-searchable-web.html">a subset of the deep web</a>, contains websites and content that require specific software or authorization to access. These hidden portions of the Internet make it challenging to account for all existing websites.</p>



<p>The prevalence of parked domains and inactive websites also adds complexity to counting websites. Differentiating between active and inactive websites and determining their relevance to the total website isn&rsquo;t easy.</p>



<p>Given these challenges, the total number of websites on the internet can only be estimated, and these estimates are subject to continuous change.</p>



<h2 id="so-how-many-websites-are-there">So, how many websites are there?</h2>



<p>The number of websites on the Internet is constantly changing as new websites are created and old ones become inactive. The best we can do is guess, and that number can change in an instant.</p>



<p>Most publications that try to answer this question cite Siteefy as an authoritative source. <a href="https://go.redirectingat.com/?id=111346X1569483&amp;url=https://siteefy.com/how-many-websites-are-there/&amp;xcust=2-1-1804213-1-0-0&amp;sref=https://www.pcworld.com/feed" rel="nofollow">According to Siteefy</a>, there are 1.13 billion websites on the Internet, 18% of which are active. That means there are roughly 200 million active websites at the time of writing.</p>



<p>Keep in mind though that if we were to have an exact number of websites that are on the Internet as we write this piece, that number would be wrong by the time it gets published. It&rsquo;s estimated that 10,500 new websites are created every hour, and there&rsquo;s no telling how many become inactive.</p>



<p>The digital landscape is in a constant state of flux.</p>

<h2 id="what-to-look-for-in-a-usb-c-pd-charger">What to look for in a USB-C PD charger</h2>



<p>As mentioned above, the primary requirement is that your laptop includes a USB-C port that can carry power as well as data. Older laptops that came with their own power brick might have data-only USB-C but not power-supporting USB-C.</p>



<p>USB-C laptop chargers use a certified technology called USB Power Delivery (USB PD), a specification that enables power delivery along with data over a single cable.</p>



<p>All the chargers tested here are certified by the USB Implementers Forum (USB-IF), and are from reliable, trusted brands&mdash;you&rsquo;ll find cheaper, less-well-known brands online but for safety&rsquo;s sake we recommend you chose a proven brand, such as Anker, Ugreen, Satechi, Belkin, Plugable or Aukey, as well as big brands such as HP, Dell, Google and Samsung that follow the latest safety standards.</p>



<p>Chargers can get hot, so you want to trust that yours won&rsquo;t catch fire or otherwise damage your laptop. That&rsquo;s why we avoid cheaper brands that we haven&rsquo;t heard of or tested.</p>



<p><strong>USB PD 3.0</strong> supports up to 100W of power to the laptop. <strong>USB PD 3.1</strong> can reach 240W, which is useful for larger laptops that require over 100W for charging at full speed while in use, and for fast-charging compatible laptops.</p>



<h3 id="wattage">Wattage</h3>



<p>Once you know that your laptop supports USB PD charging, the most important specification to look for when choosing a laptop charger is the amount of power it can supply to your laptop&mdash;measured in watts (W).</p>



<p>The wattage you should look for will depend on the maximum power draw of your laptop&mdash;that is, the draw when you are using a bunch of apps at the same time or pushing the graphics functions, not when you&rsquo;re just browsing the web or sending emails. This level of maximum power is what the manufacturer considers when it decides on the wattage of the charger it includes with the laptop.</p>



<p>When idle or doing mundane jobs that draw only a small amount of power from the charger, laptops can recharge fully on a lower-wattage charger&mdash;so a 45W charger can top up an idle 65W laptop but much slower than a 65W charger.</p>



<p>That said, when buying a new USB-C charger, buy at least the same wattage as the charger that shipped with your laptop.</p>



<p>Smaller laptops may require just a 30W or 45W charger, while medium laptops usually need 65W, and 100W is required for larger 15-inch and larger notebooks.</p>



<p>Don&rsquo;t worry if your laptop requires just 45W and you fancy that 100W charger&mdash;using a higher-power charger won&rsquo;t harm your lower-powered laptop as it will regulate the power from the charger.</p>



<p>Using a higher-wattage charger than your laptop requires is recommended when choosing a charger with multiple ports, as the stated wattage is the maximum output, and charging other devices at the same time will start lowering the power going to the laptop.</p>



<p>For more charging questions answered, see our laptop charging FAQ at the end of this article.</p>

<h3 id="multiple-ports-for-more-devices">Multiple ports for more devices</h3>



<p>Standard laptop chargers have just one USB-C port or connector, but many third-party USB-C chargers boast multiple ports&mdash;sometimes both USB-C and older USB-A.</p>



<p>These are useful if you want to charge more than just your laptop&mdash;with multiple ports on your charger, you can power up your phone, tablet, or headphones at the same time as your laptop.</p>



<p>If you want to use more than one charging port, consider your maximum requirement&mdash;say, 65W for the laptop and another 15W for a phone and maybe 5W for a smartwatch or headphones. A charger with a higher wattage can distribute the excess power to other connected devices at the same time as you power your laptop.</p>



<p>Multi-port chargers will have a variety of charging options between ports and devices.</p>



<h3 id="go-for-gan">Go for GaN</h3>



<p>Look for chargers built with modern Gallium Nitride (GaN) technology that runs cooler than older silicon tech, meaning that devices built with it can charge devices faster and with less heat dissipation than traditional chargers and are noticeably smaller, too.</p>



<p>Newer GaN 2 can operate at even higher voltages and temperatures.</p>



<p>Dimensions given in our reviews are for the U.S. models, which are also pictured for each product, but UK models are mostly also available. Due to their sturdy three-pin Type G plug, UK models are usually wider than their U.S. Type A and B plug counterparts.</p>



<h3 id="fast-charging">Fast charging&nbsp;</h3>



<p>Modern laptops support fast charging, where the battery can be pumped up to at least 50 percent capacity&mdash;sometimes greater&mdash;in around 30 minutes.</p>



<p>A laptop with fast-charging support is easier to keep close to a full charge. If the battery is fully drained, fast charging helps you get back to work fast, and you can quickly top up the battery when required&mdash;even just a few minutes of charging can save you when you don&rsquo;t have much time near a power socket.</p>

<h3 id="choose-the-right-charging-cable">Choose the right charging cable</h3>



<p>Some USB-C laptop chargers ship with a cable, but most don&rsquo;t. Make sure the cable you use supports power delivery, as some are for data-transfer only.</p>



<p>The wisest choices for USB PD cables are based on the latest Thunderbolt 4 or USB4 standards, which are both backwards compatible with Thunderbolt 3 and USB-C, and supports both charging and up to 40Gbps data transfer. But Thunderbolt/USB4 is more expensive and often available in shorter lengths than USB-C cables.</p>



<p>Look for a USB-C PD or Thunderbolt cable. If you want to charge at over 100W, this should support USB PD 3.1. Not all cables are marked as supporting 240W so make sure you check the PD rating before your purchase.</p>

<h3 id="wall-charger-or-desktop-charger">Wall charger or desktop charger</h3>



<p>A <strong>wall charger</strong> features plug prongs and connects directly into the wall power socket. Straight-to-socket wall chargers are small and portable&mdash;most will fit in your pocket. They might not have as many ports as a desktop charger but can still have several ports to choose from.</p>



<p>If you carry your charger around with you a lot, look for a wall charger that has foldable plug prongs, which makes it more comfortable and smaller. These are more common with U.S. Type A plugs than UK Type G plugs.</p>



<p>A <strong>desktop charger</strong> is separated from the socket by a power cable (usually detachable), with the charger instead sitting on your desk. Desktop chargers always feature multiple ports, and sometimes a separate power supply that will hide under the desk.</p>



<p>They are great for keeping your device-charging organized and in one neat package but are usually less portable than wall chargers.</p>



<p>There are also handy <strong>power strips</strong> with USB PD ports as well as extra sockets&mdash;useful in a home or office environment with few power sockets.</p>

<h3 id="can-i-use-a-charger-with-a-lower-wattage-than-my-laptop">Can I use a charger with a lower wattage than my laptop?</h3>



<div class="wp-block-idg-base-theme-faq-answer-block how-to-tip">
<p>Yes, but you should try not to. You can charge a 100W laptop with a 65W charger, for example, but it&rsquo;s not advisable as the laptop battery could drain faster than it is being charged, especially if in use at the time.</p>



<p>Charging an idle laptop should mean that its battery will be fully charged with a lower-wattage charger, but it will take much longer than with the correctly rated charger. As a one-off it should be fine, but we do not recommend it in the long term. Using a lower-rated charger could result in overheating, which is never a good thing with electronics.</p>
</div>
</div></div>



<div class="wp-block-idg-base-theme-faq-inner-block faq-save-block"><div class="faq-save-content"><span class="faq-rank">6.</span>
<h3 id="will-my-laptop-charge-faster-if-not-in-use">Will my laptop charge faster if not in use?</h3>



<div class="wp-block-idg-base-theme-faq-answer-block how-to-tip">
<p>Yes. When your laptop is idle or you are using only light apps such as email, it will be draining only a small amount of power from its battery, so it will charge faster. If you are using performance-hungry apps at the same time as charging, the charger will take longer to catch up as the laptop&rsquo;s battery is constantly draining while in use. &nbsp;</p>
</div>
</div></div>



<div class="wp-block-idg-base-theme-faq-inner-block faq-save-block"><div class="faq-save-content"><span class="faq-rank">7.</span>
<h3 id="can-i-charge-my-phone-using-a-laptop-charger">Can I charge my phone using a laptop charger?</h3>



<div class="wp-block-idg-base-theme-faq-answer-block how-to-tip">
<p>Yes, you can use a laptop charger to charge your phone. Some chargers feature more than one port, and the lower wattage ports are perfect for phone charging&mdash;but you can also charge a phone with a 140W charging port if you want to, although it&rsquo;s safer for the phone if you use a lower-wattage port.</p>
</div>
</div></div>



<div class="wp-block-idg-base-theme-faq-inner-block faq-save-block"><div class="faq-save-content"><span class="faq-rank">8.</span>
<h3 id="can-i-use-any-cable-to-charge-a-laptop">Can I use any cable to charge a laptop?</h3>



<div class="wp-block-idg-base-theme-faq-answer-block how-to-tip">
<p>No. You must use a USB-C cable that supports power delivery. Some USB-C cables can&rsquo;t carry as much power as others. A USB PD 3.0 cable can carry 100W of power. A USB PD 3.1 cable can carry 240W of power.</p>
</div>
</div></div>



<div class="wp-block-idg-base-theme-faq-inner-block faq-save-block"><div class="faq-save-content"><span class="faq-rank">9.</span>
<h3 id="can-i-use-a-ubc-c-cable-to-charge-a-thunderbolt-or-usb4-laptop">Can I use a UBC-C cable to charge a Thunderbolt or USB4 laptop?</h3>



<div class="wp-block-idg-base-theme-faq-answer-block how-to-tip">
<p>Yes. Thunderbolt 3, Thunderbolt 4, and USB4 are backwards compatible with USB-C for data and charging.</p>



<p>Thunderbolt and USB4 also have higher data-transfer bandwidth&mdash;up to 40Gbps&mdash;than USB-C, which is rated between 5Gbps and 20Gbps.</p>
</div>
</div></div>



<div class="wp-block-idg-base-theme-faq-inner-block faq-save-block"><div class="faq-save-content"><span class="faq-rank">10.</span>
<h3 id="can-i-use-a-thunderbolt-cable-with-a-usb-c-charger">Can I use a Thunderbolt cable with a USB-C charger?</h3>



<div class="wp-block-idg-base-theme-faq-answer-block how-to-tip">
<p>Yes. Thunderbolt is backwards compatible with USB-C so a Thunderbolt cable can be used with a USB-C laptop or USB-C charger.</p>



<p>You still need to ensure that the Thunderbolt cable can handle the right amount of power you require. All Thunderbolt 3 or 4 and USB4 cables can handle 100W of power, but some support up to 240W.</p>
</div>
</div></div>



<div class="wp-block-idg-base-theme-faq-inner-block faq-save-block"><div class="faq-save-content"><span class="faq-rank">11.</span>
<h3 id="should-i-charge-my-laptop-to-100">Should I charge my laptop to 100%?</h3>



<div class="wp-block-idg-base-theme-faq-answer-block how-to-tip">
<p>Only if you need to. If you are going on a road trip and need all the juice your laptop battery can supply, then by all means charge it to 100 percent before heading out.</p>



<p>But it&rsquo;s easier on the battery if you keep it charged between 40 and 80 percent&mdash;no lower and no higher if you can.</p>



<p>Batteries charged to the full 100 percent might have only 300-500 discharge cycles, but batteries charged only up to 80 percent could get to nearly 4x that number of recharging cycles.</p>
</div>
</div></div>



<div class="wp-block-idg-base-theme-faq-inner-block faq-save-block"><div class="faq-save-content"><span class="faq-rank">12.</span>
<h3 id="should-i-fully-drain-my-laptop-battery-to-0">Should I fully drain my laptop battery to 0%?</h3>



<div class="wp-block-idg-base-theme-faq-answer-block how-to-tip">
<p>No. As with our advice not to fully charge a lithium-ion laptop battery too often, it&rsquo;s better for long-term battery life if you keep it charged between 40 and 80 percent.</p>
