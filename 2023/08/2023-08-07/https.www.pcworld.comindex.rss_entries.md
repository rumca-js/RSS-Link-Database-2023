<p>Easy Firewall, available for $25, is not a standalone firewall, but instead a helpful graphical user interface for the complex Windows Firewall already included with your PC. </p>



<p>Easy Firewall lists which applications have received permissions to send data to the internet or receive data from there. If an app tries to obtain rights that it did not have before, you about it in real time and can decide for yourself whether you want to allow it.</p>



<p>You can also change the existing rules with a few mouse clicks. For example, some programs require Internet access to enable activation, but do not actually need access to the network afterwards. Easy Firewall makes changing that access, well, easy. The tool uses a dialogue box to explain, without any technical jargon, what each setting means, though there is still a definite learning curve involved with wrapping your head around Windows Firewall management.</p>



<p>A convenient feature is the <strong>panic mode</strong>: With a mouse click, all Internet connections can immediately be cut. It&rsquo;s even faster than, say, using Windows network settings to disconnect your PC from your network. If you suspect you&rsquo;re under attack, it&rsquo;s a handy feature.</p>



<p><strong>Alternative:</strong> If you&rsquo;re looking for more control over how the Windows Firewall behaves, but aren&rsquo;t sure whether you want to spend $25 on Easy Firewall&rsquo;s straightforward design and presentation, Binisoft&rsquo;s free tool <a href="https://go.redirectingat.com/?id=111346X1569483&amp;url=https://www.binisoft.org/wfc&amp;xcust=2-2-2020912-1-0-0&amp;sref=https://www.pcworld.com/feed" rel="nofollow" target="_blank">Windows Firewall Control</a> offers similar features.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="conclusion">Conclusion</h2>



<p>Easy Firewall simplifies the handling of the Windows Firewall, which otherwise works largely automatically. It may be overkill for a lot of people &mdash; Windows Firewall tends to handle its job well despite being in the background &mdash; but for more technically inclined users, it should be informative to learn which programs establish network connections at all, and how they do so. Whether the Windows Firewall would have done its job better or worse without your intervention may vary from case to case and is hard to gauge, however.</p>

## Want more Windows themes? Check out the Microsoft Store
 - [https://www.pcworld.com/article/2022424/windows-10-11-themes-microsoft-store.html](https://www.pcworld.com/article/2022424/windows-10-11-themes-microsoft-store.html)
 - RSS feed: https://www.pcworld.com/index.rss
 - date published: 2023-08-07T17:08:28+00:00

<div id="link_wrapped_content">
<section class="wp-block-bigbite-multi-title"><div class="container"></div></section>



<p>You can brighten up your Windows 11 desktop with the so-called themes. These are unified combinations of background image(s), colors for the various interface elements (like the taskbar and title bar), new sound files, and changes to the mouse pointer and other elements, all enabled at once with the click of a button.</p>



<p>That said, there are only a few native Windows 11 themes to choose from in the Settings app under <em>Personalization &gt; Themes</em>. Microsoft offers you many, many more if you click on the link &ldquo;Get more themes from the Microsoft Store&rdquo; (Windows 10) or on the button &ldquo;Browse themes&rdquo; to the right (Windows 11). The Microsoft Store will then open and list a considerable number of designs, some free and some you have to pay for.</p>



<p>You&rsquo;ll have dozens of themes to scroll through. Once you have chosen one, click on it and continue with <em>Get &gt; Install &gt; Open</em>. Now it can be selected in the Windows Settings app and integrated into the system. </p>


<div class="extendedBlock-wrapper block-coreImage undefined"><figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img alt="Windows Designs" class="wp-image-1977105" height="780" src="https://b2c-contenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/07/pcw07_Designs.jpg?quality=50&amp;strip=all" width="919" /></figure><p class="imageCredit">IDG</p></div>



<p>Microsoft also makes themes <a href="https://go.redirectingat.com/?id=111346X1569483&amp;url=https://support.microsoft.com/en-us/windows/desktop-themes-94880287-6046-1d35-6d2f-35dee759701e&amp;xcust=2-1-2022424-1-0-0&amp;sref=https://www.pcworld.com/feed" rel="nofollow">available for download</a> directly from the Internet.</p>



<p>On the website you&rsquo;ll find the designs sorted by theme with descriptions, but there is unfortunately no visual preview. When you click on a design, the browser saves it in the download directory on your hard drive, then can be installed with a double click. Windows automatically adds designs in theme pack format to the selectable themes in the Windows Settings app after you click on them. </p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="what-to-look-for-in-a-windows-hello-webcam">What to look for in a Windows Hello webcam</h2>



<p><a href="https://www.pcworld.com/article/422745/hands-on-with-windows-10s-windows-hello-it-just-works.html">Windows Hello dates back to 2015</a>, and the introduction of Windows 10 running on Microsoft&rsquo;s Surface hardware. It replaces the need to type your password to log in every time you sit down at your desk, providing a speedy yet secure compromise to secure your PC.</p>



<p>Typically, Windows Hello is associated with depth-camera technology, which recognizes the various contours of your face, identifying you and only you.  (A normal webcam doesn&rsquo;t understand that it&rsquo;s looking at a 3D image.) The image is then compared with a reference image which is stored during setup. The process itself is fast: It takes just a second or two for a Windows Hello webcam to recognize you, verify your identity to the PC it&rsquo;s connected to via a USB cable, and log you in. Windows Hello can also read your fingerprint with a separate Windows Hello fingerprint reader, but these webcams are all you need.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="1080p">1080p </h3>



<p>The resolution of the webcam matters. Most laptops still ship with 720p webcams, and a higher-resolution webcam will make you look better than your colleagues.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="fixed-focus-versus-autofocus">Fixed-focus versus autofocus </h3>



<p>A worthwhile but not critical feature, autofocus will keep you in focus if you move back and forth, walk around the room, or so on. Fixed-focus webcams usually work just fine for normal use.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="auto-exposure">Auto-exposure </h3>



<p>Most webcams will adjust their exposure for the available lighting, but superior webcams will do so in a way that makes you look well-lit and not a webcam zombie.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="noise-cancellation">Noise cancellation </h3>



<p>Running a fan in the background? Are the neighbors mowing their lawn or blowing their leaves away? A good noise-cancelling webcam with a mic will filter out these background noises.</p>

<div class="wp-block-idg-base-theme-faq-answer-block how-to-tip">
<p>After buying a webcam, make sure you have a Windows 10 or Windows 11 PC. Connect the webcam to the PC via the USB cable and that should be all you need for basic webcam functionality. <p>When that screen appears, you&rsquo;ll need to add a PIN or password as a backup. Be sure that your PC is using your webcam (though it should by default). Windows Hello setup requires simply looking at the screen while the webcam looks at you and records your image.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading toc" id="h-die-tucke-der-neuen-technologien">The pitfall of new technologies</h2>



<p>In the past few years, Nvidia has introduced one new technology after another and ultimately (at least currently) won the duel against AMD. With ray tracing, Nvidia has set a graphics trend that will probably be with us for some time to come and in which Team Green is a pioneer. The DLSS upscaling technology is still ahead of the AMD and Intel alternatives.</p>



<p>However, it is becoming more and more of a trend that these innovations are not really backwards compatible&mdash;and that is a disaster from the consumer&rsquo;s point of view. For example, said Deep Learning Super Sampling (DLSS), whose latest version 3 including frame generation is only compatible with Nvidia&rsquo;s current 40-series cards.</p>



<p>So if you bought a 20- or 30-series card in the past years, possibly for a lot of money given the graphics card prices of the last few years, you are now already looking down the tube.</p>



<p>There are of course technical reasons why these new technologies do not work on old cards. Nevertheless, as a consumer I don&rsquo;t like the feeling when an expensive product is consigned to the antique department by the manufacturer after only a few years. Especially since there is another way, as AMD shows.</p>

<p>Even though Nvidia has good arguments on its side from a technical point of view, AMD has not been idle in recent years either&mdash;and is pursuing a completely different strategy. AMD makes its in-house technologies available to as many users as possible. <p>It becomes even clearer when comparing FSR against DLSS. While AMD&rsquo;s technology is compatible even with competitor graphics cards from Nvidia and Intel, Nvidia not infrequently excludes GPUs from its own house when it comes to new functions. For example, DLSS only works when using an RTX GPU, older GTX models are left out in the cold, and DLSS 3 Frame Generation only works with the current RTX 40-series graphics cards. AMD relies on industry standards and open source solutions, while Nvidia implements proprietary solutions.</p>



<p>It felt like Nvidia was constantly bringing new features to the market without really addressing the needs of existing customers. Team Red may be a little behind in the latest high-tech features, but it relies on open source technologies and doesn&rsquo;t have to hide at all in fundamental areas like rasterization performance and memory.</p>



<p>And even if ray tracing is now implemented in more and more games, without ray tracing you can play any game, but without rasterization you can&rsquo;t play any game&mdash;apart from a few exceptions with path tracing. </p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="the-2022-security-breaches">The 2022 security breaches</h2>


<div class="extendedBlock-wrapper block-coreImage undefined"><figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img alt="Screenshot of LastPass security bulletin" class="wp-image-2014018" height="800" src="https://b2c-contenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/lastpass.png?w=1200" width="1200" /></figure><p class="imageCredit">PCWorld</p></div>



<p>LastPass&rsquo;s disclosures about its 2022 security breaches was like watching a train wreck in slow motion. First came the <a href="https://www.pcworld.com/article/917244/lastpass-got-hacked-but-you-werent-2.html">initial announcement in August</a>, which claimed that no customer data was affected&mdash;just a developer environment. Then <a href="https://www.pcworld.com/article/1419901/lastpass-got-hacked-again-and-this-time-your-data-got-taken.html">three months later</a> came an update that customer data <em>was</em> affected. Nearly a month after that, the company revealed that customer information and password vaults had been stolen. Not only that, but elements in those vaults (including URLs) <a href="https://go.redirectingat.com/?id=111346X1569483&amp;url=https://support.lastpass.com/help/what-data-was-accessed&amp;xcust=2-1-2013960-1-0-0&amp;sref=https://www.pcworld.com/feed" rel="nofollow">had not been encrypted</a>.</p>



<p>As mentioned above, LastPass was no stranger to security incidents before this breach, but none were as shocking as this one. Customers of online password managers generally trust that their service is safeguarded enough that their data&mdash;even if encrypted&mdash;can&rsquo;t be accessed by unauthorized parties. Hearing after a breach that vault data was unencrypted was a bit blindsiding.&nbsp;</p>



<p>And perhaps there&rsquo;s good reason from an engineering perspective for why some details&mdash;like URLs, how often you use an entry, when you last updated an entry, etc&mdash;would not be encrypted. But that brings us to the second way LastPass skewered my trust in them, which is&hellip;</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="bad-communication">Bad communication</h2>


<div class="extendedBlock-wrapper block-coreImage undefined"><figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img alt="LastPass password iteration upgrade email" class="wp-image-2014053" height="977" src="https://b2c-contenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/lastpass2.png" width="838" /></figure><p class="imageCredit">PCWorld</p></div>



<p>So, obviously, I don&rsquo;t know what it takes to run a business where you&rsquo;re not only safeguarding really sensitive info, but you&rsquo;re actively dealing with threats to that info on a regular basis.&nbsp;</p>

<p>Let&rsquo;s take a recent example. In mid-July, I logged in to make a last check of the account I was abandoning, only to see a message that my password iterations had been raised to 600,000.&nbsp;</p>

<p>A higher number of password iterations is in theory a good thing. It&rsquo;s supposed to help slow down the ability to quickly guess what your password is. Modern cryptography standards recommends 600,000 iterations, which is probably why LastPass chose to boost customers universally to that level.&nbsp;</p>



<p>But this happened in July 2023. That is, six months after the disclosure in December about everyone&rsquo;s vault data being stolen. A half year passed in which people who did not check that setting back in December (like I did) and increased it (like I also did) were left with much lower iterations (like mine was before I fiddled with it).</p>



<p>It says a lot that my first thought was, &ldquo;<em>What kind of security issue did they have </em>this time<em> to prompt this?</em>&rdquo; Also, that my second one was, &ldquo;<em>Why is this happening now?</em>&rdquo;</p>



<p>The email explaining this change came several hours after I made a quick online search to figure out just what the heck was going on. Then another copy came in the next day. The contents did not explain the timing nor the motivating reason behind the increase.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="the-web-interface-is-disappointing">The web interface is disappointing</h2>


<div class="extendedBlock-wrapper block-coreImage undefined"><figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img alt="LastPass web interface" class="wp-image-2014076" height="542" src="https://b2c-contenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/lastpass3.png?w=1200" width="1200" /></figure><p class="imageCredit">PCWorld</p></div>



<p>Once upon a time, LastPass&rsquo;s web interface was reasonably decent. Maybe not the slickest, but it felt modern enough.</p>



<p>Nowadays, it feels much more bare-bones compared to rival password managers. Small changes over time has degraded the web interface, too. My biggest beef is that it relies heavily on persistent cookies to maintain settings. Incognito browsing means that your layout will never remain saved&mdash;it always reverts the view to LastPass&rsquo;s default.&nbsp;</p>



<p>Banner messages appear repeatedly, too. Perhaps this is petty of me, but when a permanent banner message appeared for the browser extension appeared, that was when I finally reached my limit. LastPass has the logs of what devices I&rsquo;ve used and my consistent, unrelenting use of the web interface for years and years. Being nagged persistently is not going to make me change that habit.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="exporting-your-vault-is-a-nightmare">Exporting your vault is a nightmare</h2>


<div class="extendedBlock-wrapper block-coreImage undefined"><figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img alt="LastPass export confirmation email" class="wp-image-2014081" height="560" src="https://b2c-contenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/lastpass4.png" width="845" /><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">Strangely, exporting through web interface requires going through a verification process, but the browser extension will cough up the CSV immediately.</figcaption></figure><p class="imageCredit">PCWorld</p></div>



<p>This section was filled with far saltier language until I remembered you all (and my editor) would be reading it. Roll up your sleeves, because we&rsquo;re getting into the dirty details with this one.</p>



<p>You&rsquo;d think that perhaps, if you were leaving a service, the business would be incentivized to make the process as easy as possible&mdash;thereby increasing the chances you might return someday. LastPass tries for this, but it doesn&rsquo;t do it consistently. And lucky me, I got caught up in whatever development hole that allows for sloppy password exports.</p>



<p>Generally, when you switch password managers, you&rsquo;ll export your vault data to a CSV or XML file. They&rsquo;re basic file formats that can be easily read across different programs (in theory, anyway). LastPass only exports to CSV for this purpose and the defining characteristic of the <a href="https://go.redirectingat.com/?id=111346X1569483&amp;url=https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comma-separated_values&amp;xcust=2-1-2013960-1-0-0&amp;sref=https://www.pcworld.com/feed" rel="nofollow">comma separated values</a> format is that (as you&rsquo;d expect from the name), commas are used to indicate separate data fields.</p>



<p><em>Note: If exporting all your passwords to an unencrypted format like CSV or XML, saving it to </em><a href="https://www.pcworld.com/article/549444/how-to-encrypt-files-in-windows.html"><em>an encrypted folder on your PC</em></a><em> will help safeguard them as you transition between LastPass and a new password manager.</em></p>



<p>I want to be clear&mdash;I&rsquo;m the kind of person that if something goes wonky, I like to understand why. And when my export came out a mess, with a bunch of entries containing orphaned data, I tried to make sense of what I was seeing.</p>



<p>At first, I thought the root cause was commas in the text fields. That perhaps they were causing entries to be split up and read as different entries (with data ending in the wrong fields, to boot). But that didn&rsquo;t explain why some entries with no commas at all got split up. Or why other entries were just plain missing.</p>


<div class="extendedBlock-wrapper block-coreImage undefined"><figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img alt="LastPass partial export from web interface" class="wp-image-2014087" height="172" src="https://b2c-contenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/export.png?w=1200" width="1200" /><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">This export through the web interface keeps commas in the right places, but three entries in this test account are missing in the CSV file. The browser extension properly exports them all.</figcaption></figure><p class="imageCredit">PCWorld</p></div>



<p>I still had no clear answers by the time I finished manually cross-checking every single entry against the originals in LastPass, a necessary evil because the data was untrustworthy, but importing and cleaning up the mess was still faster than creating all the entries from scratch in the new password manager.</p>



<p>Trying different browsers and methods of export (i.e., initiated through the web interface vs the browser extension) didn&rsquo;t clear up the confusion. Turns out the web interface does not export all entries (Firefox) or straight up returns a blank CSV file (Chrome), but both Firefox&rsquo;s web interface export and the Chrome browser extension had the same issues with data integrity. Meanwhile, when I tried exporting on a test account, the data fields for each entry came out perfect (even if some were still missing in the web export).</p>



<p>As best as I can tell, either the age of the account influences how the data is stored and parsed on the servers, or the use of certain special characters in non-password text fields triggers some kind of bug in the export script. Either way, you can&rsquo;t trust you&rsquo;re actually getting all your passwords out intact. Hours into the tedious process of salvaging my import, I seriously considered abandoning the process in favor of password resets for every service, and letting the new password manager capture them. I mean, I was going to have to do that anyway as a final precaution given the LastPass security breaches, right?</p>



<p><strong>Never again.</strong></p>

## Lenovo ThinkPad X1 Nano review: Slim and fast, but short on battery life
 - [https://www.pcworld.com/article/2016999/lenovo-thinkpad-x1-nano-review-2.html](https://www.pcworld.com/article/2016999/lenovo-thinkpad-x1-nano-review-2.html)

<div class="review" id="review-body"><span class="review-title">At a glance</span><h3 class="review-subTitle" id="experts-rating">Expert's Rating</h3><div class="starRating"></div>
<div><div class="review-columns"><div class="review-column"><h3 class="review-subTitle" id="pros">Pros</h3><ul class="pros review-list"><li>Thin, lightweight design</li><li>Excellent keyboard</li><li>Good processor performance&nbsp;</li><li>Supports Wi-Fi 6E and optional 4G LTE</li></ul></div><div class="review-column"><h3 class="review-subTitle" id="cons">Cons</h3><ul class="cons review-list"><li>Small touchpad</li><li>Limited physical connectivity</li><li>Short battery life</li></ul></div></div></div><h3 class="review-subTitle review-subTitle--borderTop" id="our-verdict">Our Verdict</h3><p class="verdict">Lenovo&rsquo;s X1 Nano packs a superb keyboard and strong processor performance despite its small size, but its battery life is behind the curve.</p>
</div>


<p>Lenovo&rsquo;s X1 Nano, now in its third generation, aims to cram an enjoyable and performant laptop into the smallest footprint possible. That means it can&rsquo;t be <em>too small</em> (that would ruin the keyboard), but it still needs to look and feel smaller than its mainstream rivals. The X1 Nano meets this goal, though it requires a few compromises.</p>

</blockquote>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="lenovo-thinkpad-x1-nano-specs-and-features">Lenovo ThinkPad X1 Nano: Specs and features</h2>



<p>The Lenovo X1 Nano&rsquo;s name implies it&rsquo;s small&mdash;and that&rsquo;s correct. It measures less than a foot across and less than .58 inches thick. It&rsquo;s also light, weighing a mere 2.18 pounds.</p>



<ul>
<li>CPU: Intel Core i7-1360P</li>



<li>Memory: 16GB LPDDR5 6400</li>



<li>Graphics/GPU: Intel Iris Xe</li>



<li>Display: 13-inch 2,160 x 1,350 IPS touchscreen</li>



<li>Storage: 512GB PCIe Gen4 SSD</li>



<li>Webcam: 1080p&nbsp;</li>



<li>Connectivity:&nbsp; 2x Thunderbolt 4 / USB 4 with Power Delivery, DisplayPort Alternate Mode, 3.5mm combo audio</li>



<li>Networking: WiFi 6E, Bluetooth 5.2, optional Nano-SIM</li>



<li>Biometrics: Fingerprint reader, IR Camera for Windows Hello facial recognition&nbsp;</li>



<li>Battery capacity: 49.6 watt-hours</li>



<li>Dimensions: 11.54 x 8.19 x .58 inches</li>



<li>Weight: 2.18 pounds</li>



<li>MSRP: $1,217 base MSRP, $1,714 as-tested</li>
</ul>



<p>However, Lenovo still equips the X1 Nano with a relatively quick Core i7-1360P processor. The &ldquo;P&rdquo; is important&mdash;Intel&rsquo;s Core i7 P-Series processors are generally quicker than the U-Series, which can lead to some confusion (the Core i7-1365U, for example, is slightly slower, as it has fewer CPU cores and a lower maximum power draw). The X1 Nano also packs a healthy 16GB of RAM and 512GB of storage, with up to 1TB of storage available in top-tier configurations.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="lenovo-thinkpad-x1-nano-design-and-build-quality">Lenovo ThinkPad X1 Nano: Design and build quality</h2>


<div class="extendedBlock-wrapper block-coreImage undefined"><figure class="wp-block-image size-large is-resized"><img alt="Lenovo Nano build quality" class="wp-image-2017030" height="807" src="https://b2c-contenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/lenovo-thinkpad-x1-nano-1-1.jpg?quality=50&amp;strip=all&amp;w=1200" width="1211" /></figure><p class="imageCredit">IDG / Matthew Smith</p></div>



<p>The Lenovo X1 Nano is a remarkably petite system even for a laptop with a 13-inch display. It&rsquo;s not as thin as Apple&rsquo;s MacBook Air 13, but smaller in both width and depth, and its footprint is a close match to the Dell XPS 13. But the X1 Nano&rsquo;s mere 2.18 pounds of weight makes it lighter than either by roughly half a pound. None of these laptops are heavy, of course, but the X1 Nano&rsquo;s relative lack of heft is noticeable in-hand.</p>



<p>This doesn&rsquo;t mean the X1 Nano is fragile, however. It feels surprisingly rigid and sturdy with minimal flex along all body panels including the display, which just barely bends while opening and closing the laptop (the same is true of most competitors though, notably, the XPS 13 and MacBook Air 13 are stiff as a board). Lenovo boasts the laptop is MIL-STD-810H tested, and while this standard doesn&rsquo;t require third-party certification, <a href="https://go.redirectingat.com/?id=111346X1569483&amp;url=https://www.lenovo.com/us/en/thinkpad-milspec/?referer=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.lenovo.com%2Fus%2Fen%2Fp%2Flaptops%2Fthinkpad%2Fthinkpadx1%2Fthinkpad-x1-nano-gen-3-%2813-inch-intel%29%2Flen101t0054%3ForgRef%3Dhttps%25253A%25252F%25252Fduckduckgo.com%25252F&amp;xcust=2-1-2016999-1-0-0&amp;sref=https://www.pcworld.com/feed" rel="nofollow">Lenovo at least mentions the 12 specific tests they perform</a>.&nbsp;</p>



<p>As for the aesthetic, well&mdash;it&rsquo;s a ThinkPad. Lenovo occasionally deviates from the tried-and-true formula with inexpensive machines like the Lenovo ThinkBook, but ThinkPads are still ThinkPads: simple, black slabs with a grippy exterior and the classic red TrackPoint nub in the center of the keyboard. It&rsquo;s not attractive, to be sure, but it remains distinctive and offers a more rugged, workmanlike alternative to the showy <a href="https://www.pcworld.com/article/1355530/dell-xps-13-9315-review.html">Dell XPS 13</a> and Apple MacBook Air 13.&nbsp;</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="lenovo-thinkpad-x1-nano-keyboard-trackpad">Lenovo ThinkPad X1 Nano: Keyboard, trackpad</h2>


<div class="extendedBlock-wrapper block-coreImage undefined"><figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img alt="Lenovo Nano keyboard" class="wp-image-2017060" height="800" src="https://b2c-contenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/lenovo-thinkpad-x1-nano-2.jpg?quality=50&amp;strip=all&amp;w=1200" width="1200" /></figure><p class="imageCredit">IDG / Matthew Smith</p></div>



<p>Lenovo has a reputation for excellent keyboards, and the X1 Nano doesn&rsquo;t disappoint. It delivers a surprisingly spacious layout and large, easy-to-find keys that are excellent for touch typing. Only a few keys along the edges, such as Backspace, are smaller than usual.&nbsp;</p>



<p>Key feel is great, too, with reasonable travel and a firm, crisp bottoming action. It&rsquo;s not quite as good as what&rsquo;s found on larger, thicker ThinkPads, but I prefer it to the keyboard found on Dell&rsquo;s XPS 13 (which, to be clear, is good&mdash;but not this good). The ThinkPad X1 Nano&rsquo;s key feel is a close match to the Apple MacBook Air 13 M2, which also has a firm, clicky keyboard.&nbsp;</p>



<p>A classic Lenovo touchpad with Trackpoint provides mouse navigation. It has an unusual design with physically separated left, right, and center mouse buttons just below the space. These buttons are meant for use with the Trackpoint nub in the center of the keyboard: when using it, they fall immediately under your thumb.&nbsp;</p>



<p>Whether you like this depends on your thoughts on the Trackpoint. Lenovo&rsquo;s red nub lets you use the mouse without shifting your hands away from the keyboard, but the Trackpoint is more sensitive and touchy than a touchpad. This design also reduces the physical size of the touchpad, which is more narrow than what you&rsquo;ll find on the Dell XPS 13, Apple MacBook AIr 13, or <a href="https://www.pcworld.com/article/1927897/asus-zenbook-s-13-oled-review.html">Asus Zenbook S 13 OLED</a>. Put simply&mdash;the Trackpoint is great, but the touchpad feels cramped.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="lenovo-thinkpad-x1-nano-display-audio">Lenovo ThinkPad X1 Nano: Display, audio</h2>


<div class="extendedBlock-wrapper block-coreImage undefined"><figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img alt="Lenovo Nano hero" class="wp-image-2017026" height="800" src="https://b2c-contenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/lenovo-thinkpad-x1-nano-5.jpg?quality=50&amp;strip=all&amp;w=1200" width="1200" /><figcaption class="wp-element-caption"> </figcaption></figure><p class="imageCredit">IDG / Matthew Smith</p></div>



<p>The Lenovo X1 Nano offers two display options. Both use an IPS display panel and have an unusual 2,160 x 1,350 resolution, but buyers have the option to add touchscreen support. I received the touchscreen, which also has an &ldquo;anti-glare, anti-smudge&rdquo; finish, but the two displays should perform similarly.&nbsp;</p>



<p>Choosing an IPS display leads to several weaknesses and one strength. First, the strength: the X1 Nano&rsquo;s display is extremely bright with a maximum of 472 nits. That&rsquo;s towards the high end for a laptop display and, when combined with the anti-reflective coat, it makes for a display that&rsquo;s usable in nearly any situation. That&rsquo;s an important trait for a small, portable laptop like the X1 Nano, since it&rsquo;s likely to be used in a corporate office, airport, and other brightly lit spaces.</p>



<p>Unfortunately, the display&rsquo;s performance otherwise disappoints. It provides a contrast ratio of just 1690:1 and spans just 78 percent of the DCI-P3 color gamut. Neither result is bad, but many modern laptops have turned towards OLED displays that, though not as bright, provide superior image quality in both entertainment and content creation. I can understand why Lenovo stuck a bright IPS display for the X1 Nano, but the trade-off in image quality is noticeable when it&rsquo;s time to kick back, relax, and watch some Netflix.&nbsp;</p>



<p>Audio quality is mediocre, though it remains acceptable for a laptop this size. The speakers&rsquo; maximum volume is only loud enough to fill a small, quiet office with sound, and the lack of low-end oomph saps drama from music and movies. Dialogue remains clear, at least.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="lenovo-thinkpad-x1-nano-webcam-microphone-biometrics">Lenovo ThinkPad X1 Nano: Webcam, microphone, biometrics</h2>


<div class="extendedBlock-wrapper block-coreImage undefined"><figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img alt="Lenovo Nano webcam" class="wp-image-2017057" height="800" src="https://b2c-contenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/lenovo-thinkpad-x1-nano-3.jpg?quality=50&amp;strip=all&amp;w=1200" width="1200" /></figure><p class="imageCredit">IDG / Matthew Smith</p></div>



<p>A 1080p webcam with built-in physical privacy shutter slips into the Lenovo X1 Nano&rsquo;s top bezel. It&rsquo;s a solid camera, providing a crisp, clear, vibrant image even in dim interior lighting. I wouldn&rsquo;t recommend it for launching a YouTube career, of course, but it&rsquo;s great for video calls.</p>



<p>The same can be said of the microphone array. It picks up extremely strong, clear dialogue even when the laptop is several feet away, or when not looking directly at the laptop. Noise cancellation was also solid, as the microphone was able to filter out the sound of my typing and (usually) minimized the annoyance of a nearby barking dog.&nbsp;</p>



<p>Lenovo offers two forms of biometric login: a fingerprint reader and an IR camera with support for facial recognition login. I personally prefer to use facial recognition over a fingerprint reader, but it&rsquo;s nice to have an option.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="lenovo-thinkpad-x1-nano-connectivity">Lenovo ThinkPad X1 Nano: Connectivity</h2>



<p>The ThinkPad X1 Nano aggressively leans into Thunderbolt 4 and USB 4. It offers two such ports, both of which support Power Delivery (for charging the laptop) and DisplayPort Alternate Mode&mdash;and, aside from a 3.5mm combo audio jack, that&rsquo;s all the ports you get.</p>



<p>This isn&rsquo;t a surprise, given the laptop&rsquo;s size and its competition. Most small, light, and expensive laptops opt for Thunderbolt 4 and USB 4, as they provide a wide range of connectivity in a handful of small, easy-to-use ports. Still, shoppers who need to connect with older peripherals should be wary. A Thunderbolt 4 / USB 4 dock or hub will be required.&nbsp;</p>



<p>Ethernet is missing, but the X1 Nano goes all-in on wireless connectivity with support for Wi-Fi 6E, Bluetooth 5.1, and an optional Nano-SIM slot for 4G LTE connectivity. The X1 Nano I tested didn&rsquo;t have the Nano-SIM installed, but Wi-FI performance was excellent. I saw sustained speeds up to the limit of my Gigabit Internet connection when using the laptop in the same room as the router. I saw speeds of up to 120 Mbps in my external office, which is located 50 feet and several walls away from my router, and had no issues with the Wi-Fi connection&rsquo;s reliability in this (or any other) location.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="lenovo-thinkpad-x1-nano-performance">Lenovo ThinkPad X1 Nano: Performance</h2>



<p>The Lenovo X1 Nano is small on the outside, but its hardware is similar to many thin-and-light laptops with larger displays (like the <a href="https://www.pcworld.com/article/2000409/lg-gram-superslim-review.html">LG Gram SuperSlim</a>). It has an Intel Core i7-1360P processor with a total of 12 cores (four performance cores and eight efficient cores) and a maximum Turbo boost speed of 5GHz. All models come with 16GB of RAM (which isn&rsquo;t user-serviceable, as it&rsquo;s soldered to the mainboard) and a PCIe Gen 4 solid state drive.&nbsp;</p>

<p>Intel&rsquo;s Core i7-1360P performs well in the Lenovo X1 Nano and led the laptop to a score of 5,654 in PCMark 10. That&rsquo;s an excellent score for a laptop with Intel Iris Xe graphics (as graphics performance is a factor in this test).</p>


<div class="extendedBlock-wrapper block-coreImage undefined"><figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img alt="Lenovo Nano Cinebench results" class="wp-image-2017067" height="751" src="https://b2c-contenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/lenovo-thinkpad-x1-nano-cinebench.png?w=1200" width="1200" /></figure><p class="imageCredit">IDG / Matthew Smith</p></div>



<p>The Lenovo X1 Nano&rsquo;s performance in Cinebench R15, a benchmark that relies on multi-threaded processor performance over a short duration, was more muted. It achieved a score of 1,717 which, although not bad, does place the X1 Nano behind the Framework Laptop 13. And Lenovo&rsquo;s own Slim 7 Pro with Ryzen 7735HS once again jets ahead of the Intel pack with a score of 1,938.</p>


<div class="extendedBlock-wrapper block-coreImage undefined"><figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img alt="Lenovo Nano 3DMark results" class="wp-image-2017068" height="752" src="https://b2c-contenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/lenovo-thinkpad-x1-nano-3dmark.png?w=1200" width="1200" /></figure><p class="imageCredit">IDG / Matthew Smith</p></div>



<p>It&rsquo;s wise not to expect much from the Lenovo X1 Nano&rsquo;s graphics. It relies on Intel&rsquo;s aging Iris Xe, which achieves a score of 1,715 in 3DMark Time Spy. This is typical for a laptop with Intel Iris Xe, but it&rsquo;s not impressive compared to even entry-level discrete graphics, which can achieve a score several times higher.&nbsp;</p>



<p>The Lenovo X1 Nano&rsquo;s overall performance is solid. The Intel Core i7-1360P does well and gives the Nano an advantage over competitors that stick with less performant Intel Core i5 and i7 U-Series processors. The lack of discrete graphics is a downside, but it&rsquo;s unreasonable to expect a 13-inch, 2.18 pound Windows laptop to deliver strong graphics performance. Those who want to play 3D games, or use a GPU to accelerate a specific app, will need to buy a larger laptop. And, that limitation aside, the Lenovo X1 Nano is a capable machine that can handle not only basic productivity (like Word and Excel) but also some more demanding tasks, like photo editing and 1080p video editing.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="lenovo-thinkpad-x1-nano-battery-life">Lenovo ThinkPad X1 Nano: Battery life</h2>



<p>While Lenovo provides good performance in a small package, it had to make tough decisions about the battery. Larger batteries take up more space and add more weight, so Lenovo sticks with a small 49.6 watt-hour battery. That&rsquo;s not a large battery for a modern Windows laptop, and it unfortunately leads to modest battery life.</p>


<div class="extendedBlock-wrapper block-coreImage undefined"><figure class="wp-block-image size-large"><img alt="Lenovo Nano battery life" class="wp-image-2017071" height="755" src="https://b2c-contenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/lenovo-thinkpad-x1-nano-battery-life.png?w=1200" width="1200" /></figure><p class="imageCredit">IDG / Matthew Smith</p></div>



<p>I saw seven hours and 10 minutes of battery life in PC World&rsquo;s standard battery test, which loops a 4K file of the short film <em>Tears of Steel</em>. Real-world use was similar, as I saw the battery dip by almost half in one three and a half-hour long browsing session that made moderate use of browser tabs. That&rsquo;s not enough to nudge the Lenovo ThinkPad X1 Nano through a full workday.&nbsp;</p>



<p>Lenovo shipped my X1 Nano with the standard 65-watt USB-C charge I&rsquo;ve found with other Lenovo laptops. It&rsquo;s a bad fit for a small, light laptop. Stranger still, Lenovo itself sells a smaller 65-watt GaN power brick, <em>and</em> it&rsquo;s less expensive on Lenovo&rsquo;s store. Lenovo would do well to start shipping the GaN power brick instead of the larger, standard power brick.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="lenovo-thinkpad-x1-nano-should-you-buy-it">Lenovo ThinkPad X1 Nano: Should you buy it?</h2>



<p>The Lenovo X1 Nano is a good option for business professionals, frequent fliers, and other on-the-go users who don&rsquo;t want the burden of a large, heavy laptop. The X1 Nano is both small and light yet it still offers an excellent keyboard and speedy processor performance. Battery life is the laptop&rsquo;s most serious flaw, and it could prove a problem if travel often leaves you without access to a power outlet. If you can work around that problem, though, the X1 Nano will prove a capable, portable machine.&nbsp;</p>
