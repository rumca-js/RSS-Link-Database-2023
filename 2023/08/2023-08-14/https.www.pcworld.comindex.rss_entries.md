<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="which-cable-do-i-need">Which cable do I need?</h2>


<div class="extendedBlock-wrapper block-coreImage undefined"><figure class="wp-block-image"><a class="zoom" href="https://images.idgesg.net/images/article/2018/12/hdmi-2985919_1920-100783896-orig.jpg" rel="nofollow"><img alt="hdmi 2985919 1920" src="https://images.idgesg.net/images/article/2018/12/hdmi-2985919_1920-100783896-large.jpg?auto=webp&amp;quality=85,70" /></a><figcaption class="wp-element-caption">
<p>You can buy an HDMI cable rated for a higher spec than you need&mdash;HDMI cables are backward compatible.</p>
</figcaption></figure></div>




<p>HDMI cables are backward compatible, so you can always buy the highest-specification cable available and call it a day. Even if it exceeds what your devices can support, it could come in handy when upgrading to newer tech.</p>



<p>You don&rsquo;t have to, of course. (Our sister site TechHive explains why in this article addressing the common question of &ldquo;<a href="https://www.techhive.com/article/3330376/do-i-need-a-4k-hdmi-cable.html">Do you need a 4K HDMI cable?</a>&rdquo;) You can save a little cash by skipping future-proofing.</p>



<p>To figure out the minimum HDMI specification you need for your setup, look up the spec supported by your output device (PC, streaming device, gaming console, etc) and your display (monitor, TV). You may see these listed under &ldquo;HDMI version&rdquo; in the product information.</p>



<p>If the two match, simply buy an HDMI cable rated for that specification. If they don&rsquo;t match, you can buy a cable that&rsquo;s rated for the lower (older) spec, since your final output will be limited by the device with the lower HDMI version.</p>



<p>Example: Your monitor is rated for HDMI 1.4b, but your graphics card can support HDMI 2.0. You&rsquo;ll only ever get the maximum resolution and frame rates of HDMI 1.4b, which are 1080p/144fps and 1440p/75Hz, plus up to 4K/60fps depending on the level of color data compression.</p>



<p>You can buy a cable that matches the higher specification, but it&rsquo;s not necessary. When you replace the lower-HDMI-version device with a brand-new piece of gear, you&rsquo;ll usually get a compatible HDMI cable in the box.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="what-to-look-for-in-a-monitor">What to look for in a monitor</h2>



<p>There are literally hundreds&mdash;and perhaps thousands&mdash;of monitors to choose from. Choosing one from the crowd can be difficult, but knowing the key traits of a monitor will help you make your decision.&nbsp;</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="resolution">Resolution </h3>



<p>Most monitors are available in 4K (3,840&times;2,160), 1440p (2,560&times;1,440) and 1080p (1,920&times;1,080) resolution. A higher resolution provides a sharper, more attractive image and is generally preferable. There&rsquo;s nothing wrong with 1080p, however. It remains the baseline and is by far the most popular resolution sold today.&nbsp;</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="connectivity">Connectivity </h3>



<p>Look for a monitor with a connection supported by your computer. HDMI and DisplayPort are the most common. USB-C is rising in popularity because it can charge connected devices, such as a laptop.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="refresh-rate">Refresh rate </h3>



<p>Most monitors have a 60Hz refresh rate, but a higher refresh rate (such as 144Hz) will make motion look smooth and crisp. This is most appealing to PC gamers, as improved motion performance is most noticeable when panning the camera in a 3D game.&nbsp;</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="adaptive-sync">Adaptive sync</h3>



<p>This technology also improves motion performance. It does so by keeping a monitor&rsquo;s refresh rate in sync with the video input from your computer. <a href="https://www.pcworld.com/article/423202/g-sync-vs-freesync-faq-how-variable-refresh-rate-displays-make-pc-games-super-smooth.html">Our guide to FreeSync and G-Sync </a>provides more detail.</p>



<p>Want to know more before you make a decision? <a href="https://www.pcworld.com/article/535837/monitor_buying_guide.html">Our monitor buying guide</a> explains the nerdy details of monitor specifications.&nbsp;</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="how-we-test-monitors">How we test monitors</h2>



<p>Our monitor guides are based on testing by freelance and staff contributors with decades of combined experience.&nbsp;</p>



<p>While we rely on our eyes for an initial impression, we thoroughly test each monitor with a SpyderXElite color calibration tool. This specialized hardware can objectively measure a monitor&rsquo;s brightness, contrast, color gamut, color accuracy, luminance and color uniformity, gamma, and many other aspects of image quality.&nbsp;</p>



<p>Using this tool helps us detect strengths and weaknesses that don&rsquo;t stand out at a glance and guarantees that a change in lighting conditions or monitor location won&rsquo;t skew our impression.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="what-size-of-monitor-should-i-buy">What size of monitor should I buy?</h3>



<div class="wp-block-idg-base-theme-faq-answer-block how-to-tip">
<p>Monitor size depends on personal preference and location.&nbsp;</p>



<p>A 24-inch monitor is fine for a desk that is around 24 inches deep, or less. Its small size will still be sufficient because the monitor is closer to you.</p>



<p>Most people, however, will want to go with a 27-inch monitor. The added size will make the monitor easy to see. This also opens up a wider range of 4K monitor options, as 4K is very rare on a 24-inch monitor.</p>



<p>Larger monitors, such as a 32-inch widescreen or 34-inch ultrawide, are great for deep desks and people who want an immersive experience. Monitors this large can serve as a TV replacement in a small room.&nbsp;</p>



<p>Displays of even larger size, such as 42-inch or 48-inch HDTVs, are best for home theater setups or an unusually large office. You&rsquo;ll want to sit four to six feet away from a display of this size.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="what-display-input-is-best-hdmi-vs-displayport-vs-usb-c">What display input is best? (HDMI vs. DisplayPort vs. USB-C)</h3>



<div class="wp-block-idg-base-theme-faq-answer-block how-to-tip">
<p>USB-C is the best display input for most people. It works by bundling DisplayPort into USB-C, so offers all the same features as DisplayPort. Yet it retains the features of USB-C including optional support for Power Delivery to charge external devices. This is why <a href="https://www.pcworld.com/article/612470/you-should-buy-a-usb-c-hub-monitor-heres-what-you-need-to-know.html">we recommend a USB-C monitor for most people</a>.</p>



<p>DisplayPort is generally second-best. It supports higher resolutions and refresh rates than HDMI. This input is particularly common on desktop computers.&nbsp;</p>



<p>HDMI comes in third due to lower resolution and refresh rate support. This is improved by HDMI 2.1, but this feature remains hard to find and often adds to a monitor&rsquo;s price.</p>



<p>All three of the options above handle 1080p at 60Hz, which is the most common resolution and refresh rate available today. It&rsquo;s also important to buy a monitor that has an input compatible with your PC.</p>

<p>Modern monitors are compatible with all recent versions of Windows and MacOS. They&rsquo;re plug-and-play, which means the monitor should display an image without the need to install driver software or mess with Windows or MacOS settings.</p>

## Should you upgrade your PC or buy a new computer? A guide
 - [https://www.pcworld.com/article/2009503/upgrade-or-buy-a-new-pc-a-guide.html](https://www.pcworld.com/article/2009503/upgrade-or-buy-a-new-pc-a-guide.html)
 - RSS feed: https://www.pcworld.com/index.rss
 - date published: 2023-08-14T10:45:00+00:00

<div id="link_wrapped_content">
<section class="wp-block-bigbite-multi-title"><div class="container"></div></section>



<p>Your PC takes more and more time to boot, browser tabs open only slowly, the videos from the last holiday cannot be edited as desired or the newly purchased game jerks across the monitor. Sooner or later, every PC reaches its performance limits, regardless of whether it&rsquo;s used in the office, for productive work, for creative applications, or for gaming. </p>



<p>However, this does not always have to result in a new purchase. Often it&rsquo;s enough to replace just one component to make the PC fit again. First of all, it&rsquo;s important to find out which component is the limiting factor in order to decide whether upgrading the computer or buying a new one makes more sense. </p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading toc" id="when-does-upgrading-your-pc-make-sense">When does upgrading your PC make sense?</h2>



<p>A desktop PC is modular. Therefore, it is usually easy to replace a component&mdash;provided you have a certain technical understanding. If you are confident enough to do the upgrade yourself or have an expert at hand, there is nothing to stop you from upgrading, although you should bear a few points in mind. </p>



<p>Upgrading a PC primarily makes sense if you can achieve a large increase in performance with the purchase of only a few components and low expenditure. For example, if your PC still has an old HDD installed, you can significantly speed up the start of the operating system and programs by purchasing an SSD without having to replace the rest of the components.</p>



<p>Or if you no longer have enough RAM, but your mainboard still has free RAM slots, you can simply install additional RAM modules. Upgrading the graphics card can also make sense if the processor is bored in games, the power supply unit is sufficiently dimensioned for a faster GPU, and the case offers enough space.</p>



<h3 class="wp-block-heading" id="advantages-of-upgrading-your-pc">Advantages of upgrading your PC:</h3>



<ul>
<li>Cheaper: Replacing just one component saves a lot of money compared to buying new</li>



<li>Saves time: You don&rsquo;t have to set up a new PC, install all the necessary programs, and move your data</li>



<li>Of course, it is also more sustainable to replace only parts of the PC and not the whole computer</li>
</ul>



<blockquote class="wp-block-quote">
<p><strong>Further reading:</strong> See our guide to the <a href="https://www.pcworld.com/article/394287/best-tools-for-pc-building.html">10 essential tools for PC building</a>.</p>
</blockquote>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading toc" id="when-does-it-make-sense-to-buy-a-new-pc">When does it make sense to buy a new PC?</h2>



<p>But there are also some arguments in favor of a new purchase. If your PC is on its last legs and lacks everything, then upgrading makes little sense. If you have to invest more than half the money you would have spent on a new PC for an upgrade, buying a new one is usually the more sensible solution in the long run. </p>



<p>Often it&rsquo;s not even possible to simply upgrade a component, such as the graphics card. Installing a faster GPU requires, firstly, that the power supply delivers enough power and has the necessary connections, and secondly, that there is enough space in the case. If one or both of these requirements are not met, it can quickly become expensive to replace even more components.</p>



<p>The same applies to upgrading the processor. Especially for motherboards with Intel chipsets, it is common that a new processor socket with new chipsets follows after two CPU generations. If you want to upgrade to a new processor, this may mean that you also need a new motherboard and, in case of doubt, new RAM. </p>



<p>Another argument for a new purchase is compatibility with current software. Windows 11, for example, requires a reasonably up-to-date processor, which, as described above, may mean that you have to upgrade significantly more components. Another plus point is the warranty, because both when you buy individual components for do-it-yourself construction and when you buy a finished PC, you get a manufacturer&rsquo;s warranty on the product. </p>

<ul>
<li>Warranty: With a new PC you get a warranty on all components, with ready-made PCs</li>



<li>Efficiency: New processors and graphics cards are usually much more efficient than their predecessors&mdash;so in the long run you reduce your electricity costs</li>



<li>The latest technologies and interfaces make the PC future-proof&mdash;and you benefit from technical progress</li>

The components that are responsible for system performance are the following:</p>



<ul>
<li>System memory/storage (HDD or SSD)</li>



<li>Main memory</li>



<li>Graphics card</li>



<li>Processor</li>



<li>CPU cooler and fan</li>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading toc" id="upgrade-your-ram">Upgrade your RAM</h2>



<p>If programs start slowly or opening new tabs in the browser takes a noticeable amount of time, this is an indication that the RAM is no longer sufficient. The Windows Task Manager can be used to check whether this is the case. </p>



<p>The quickest way to open the task manager is to use the key combination <strong>Ctrl + Shift + Esc</strong><em>.</em> In the task manager, switch to the <em>Performance </em>tab and then to <em>Memory.</em> There you can read the percentage utilization of the installed RAM. If the value is 90 percent or higher, you should definitely consider upgrading the RAM. </p>



<p>Even for an office PC, we recommend at least 8GB RAM nowadays, better 16GB. We also recommend 16GB for a gaming PC, although 32GB can&rsquo;t hurt here, depending on the title. The same applies if you use your PC for creative work, such as video editing or 3D renderings.</p>



<p>Before you do this, however, you should clarify a few important questions: What RAM capacity and speed does the built-in processor support? How many RAM slots does the motherboard have and are there any free slots? Which RAM modules are currently installed&mdash;the DDR type, clock rates and latencies are of particular interest here. </p>

<p>Once you have identified the graphics card as the limiting factor, the question must be asked whether an upgrade makes sense. <p>Last but not least, you should check which PCIe generation your processor and motherboard support. PCIe is backwards compatible, which means that you can use a graphics card with PCIe 4.0 in a PCIe 3.0 slot, but this will lead to a loss of performance.</p>

<p>The speakers sound decent. It&rsquo;s a user-facing speaker system, so the sound is coming right at you. When I played one of my favorite <a href="https://go.redirectingat.com/?id=111346X1569483&amp;url=https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pIVa5xjArsk&amp;xcust=2-1-2007550-1-0-0&amp;sref=https://www.pcworld.com/feed" rel="nofollow">Lofi channels on YouTube</a>, it was loud enough to fill my dining room, which is a medium-sized space that stems off of my kitchen. The speakers use Waves Max Audio, which is supposed to enhance the overall audio performance. The speakers are fine for playing music and whatnot, but I wasn&rsquo;t super blown away or anything. It&rsquo;s definitely not like being in an IMAX theatre. </p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="lenovo-ideapad-slim-3-chromebook-webcam">Lenovo IdeaPad Slim 3 Chromebook: Webcam</h2>


<div class="extendedBlock-wrapper block-coreImage undefined"><figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img alt="Lenovo Slim 3 webcam" class="wp-image-2012178" height="683" src="https://b2c-contenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/07/lenovo-slim-3-webcam.jpg?quality=50&amp;strip=all" width="1024" /></figure><p class="imageCredit">IDG / Ashley Biancuzzo</p></div>



<p>I&rsquo;m of the mind that all laptops should come with 1080p webcams, as remote work is more common these days. Unfortunately, the Slim 3&rsquo;s 720p webcam is a real sticking point for me. As someone who works from home, I&rsquo;d prefer to look as crisp as possible during videoconferencing calls. When I recorded a video of myself typing away on the keyboard, I was disappointed in the quality of the picture. The straps of my purple dress looked washed out and background details like the swaying tree outside my window appeared a bit fuzzy. That said, you could always pick up an <a href="https://www.pcworld.com/article/393756/1080p-4k-webcam-buying-guide.html">external webcam</a>.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="lenovo-ideapad-slim-3-chromebook-connectivity">Lenovo IdeaPad Slim 3 Chromebook: Connectivity</h2>


<div class="extendedBlock-wrapper block-coreImage undefined"><figure class="wp-block-image size-full"><img alt="Lenovo Slim 3 ports" class="wp-image-2012180" height="683" src="https://b2c-contenthub.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/07/lenovo-slim-3-ports.jpg?quality=50&amp;strip=all" width="1024" /></figure><p class="imageCredit">IDG / Ashley Biancuzzo</p></div>



<p>The port selection is fine, but nothing real noteworthy. You&rsquo;re getting one Kensington lock slot, one USB-C 3.2 Gen 1, one USB-A 3.2 Gen 1, one headphone/microphone jack, and one microSD card reader. It&rsquo;s enough for the everyday person, as some folks prefer plugging into an external monitor or mouse. It&rsquo;s good to have options, yeah? You probably don&rsquo;t need to bring an adapter with you.</p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="lenovo-ideapad-slim-3-chromebook-performance">Lenovo IdeaPad Slim 3 Chromebook: Performance</h2>



<p>Look, I&rsquo;m going to level with you here. The Slim 3 isn&rsquo;t the most powerful laptop I&rsquo;ve ever tested at PCWorld, but that&rsquo;s not what it was designed for. If you&rsquo;re using the device for general-use purposes (browsing social media, checking email, and so on), then it&rsquo;s entirely serviceable. Anything more demanding than that and you&rsquo;ll likely see a bit of a slowdown. When I had a bunch of tabs open in my browser, I did notice a slight slowdown when loading up a fresh page or navigating to another open tab. That&rsquo;s not surprising, as there&rsquo;s only 4GB of RAM. RAM directly impacts web browsing speed, which is why I recommend at least 8GB for casual use. </p>



<p>Here&rsquo;s how the Lenovo IdeaPad Slim 3 Chromebook performed in our browser-based benchmarks:</p>



<ul>
<li>CrXPRT 2: 73</li>



<li>Speedometer: 29.6</li>



<li>Basemark Web 3.0: 302.97</li>



<li>Kraken: 2137.8ms</li>



<li>Jetstream 2: 74.077</li>
</ul>



<p>Compared to Chromebooks with Intel or AMD processors, the Slim 3 falls behind (but not by much). The Asus Chromebook CM34 Flip, for example, had a CrXPRT 2 score of 148 and a Speedometer score of 73.7. The Flip came equipped with an AMD processor, which is more powerful than the Slim&rsquo;s MediaTek Kompanio 520. That said, MediaTek CPUs are designed to fit inside light and portable laptops, which the Slim 3 definitely is. So, you&rsquo;ve got to pick your poison here. Either you go with a slightly slower but more portable laptop or a heavier one with zippier performance.</p>



<p>By the way, the CrXPRT 2 performance benchmark measures things like how fast your Chromebook completes tasks and the Speedometer benchmark captures the responsiveness of the web applications. These benchmarks are good indicators of how a Chromebook will perform under different loads. It should also be noted that because Chromebooks get updated every six weeks, the test results aren&rsquo;t always going to line up perfectly. </p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="lenovo-ideapad-slim-3-chromebook-battery-life">Lenovo IdeaPad Slim 3 Chromebook: Battery life</h2>



<p>The Slim 3&rsquo;s 47 watt-hour battery kicked some serious butt. When I ran the battery benchmark, which cycles through various tasks until the laptop dies, the Chromebook breathed its last metaphorical breath at the 16 hour mark. That&rsquo;s a great result, especially if you plan on taking this laptop everywhere with you. However, generally speaking, Chromebooks tend to have great battery life, so I&rsquo;m not surprised by the result. </p>



<h2 class="wp-block-heading" id="lenovo-ideapad-slim-3-chromebook-should-you-buy-it">Lenovo IdeaPad Slim 3 Chromebook: Should you buy it?</h2>



<p>The Lenovo IdeaPad Slim 3 is a perfectly serviceable Chromebook. Although it&rsquo;s not the most innovative laptop I&rsquo;ve ever worked with, it&rsquo;s still a good value buy, especially if you&rsquo;re a student or young professional. It has enough power for light use and the battery will keep you going long after your workday ends. The inclusion of a 1080p touch display is a nice feature to have on a sub-$400 laptop. Performance could be a touch snappier, but that&rsquo;s just my own personal nitpick. You&rsquo;ll need to taper your expectations some, especially as this machine has only 4GB of RAM. If you need an inexpensive everyday machine, then the IdeaPad Slim 3 Chromebook is worth your hard-earned cash.</p>
